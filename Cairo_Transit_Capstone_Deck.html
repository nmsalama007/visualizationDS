Problem Statement

Two transit realities, unevenly distributed.

Cairo's public transport system includes metro, future rail projects, formal bus routes, and a large informal ground-transport layer. The network is not equally distributed: some districts have strong rail access while peripheral and newly developed areas still depend heavily on ground or informal transport.

Established city

Dense rail and formal bus coverage; mature stop network; short distances to nearest station for most residents.

Peripheral districts

Heavy reliance on informal microbuses and minibuses; longer distances to rail; gaps in formal coverage.

New cities & NAC

Rapid urban growth absorbing residents; rail and feeder connectivity still under construction; long-term planning question.

Central question. How can the current and future rail network improve city-wide accessibility, reduce dependency on overlapping ground routes, and support evidence-based decisions for future station locations?

Related Work

Positioning this project in the literature.

Multimodal-transit accessibility analysis has a strong evidence base. The selected studies frame our methodological choices: graph integration of rail with ground/informal layers, before/after accessibility, and clustering for candidate-area interpretation.

Pereira et al. — MDPI Sustainability, 2019
BRT in Lima significantly improved job accessibility for low-income residents in peripheral areas unreached by metro — the same access profile observed in Cairo's peripheral and informal-dependent corridors.
Jittrapirom et al. — Springer ETRR, 2023
MaaS-style integration of informal operators as feeder services improved overall network coverage without eliminating existing livelihoods — directly relevant to overlap and feeder-redesign interpretation.
Yap et al. — Springer GeoJournal, 2025
First/last-mile infrastructure quality is the primary determinant of whether underserved populations actually benefit from new rail investment — informs our station-candidate interpretation.
World Bank GCR Multimodal Strategy — TfC, 2019
BRT is positioned as the designated complement to Metro in high-demand corridors outside rail catchments — the empirical baseline this analysis quantifies for Cairo.
Cervero — Springer, 2014
Foundational framework for informal transit formalization and feeder integration in megacities.
Singh & Srivastava — MDPI, 2024
Last-mile infrastructure investment as a precondition for rail-station catchment realization in peripheral urban areas.

Data Sources & Network Inputs

Five integrated inputs.

Spatial infrastructure, GTFS operations, district indicators, and OD survey demand. Integrated to evaluate coverage, identify accessibility improvements, analyze rail-ground overlap, and support clustering-based station recommendations.

Rail transport routes

Metro, Monorail, LRT, and High-Speed Rail geometries; present and future status; used to build rail network edges.

Rail transport stops

Station locations and names; used to build rail nodes and detect interchange points across lines.

GTFS ground transport

Stops, routes, trips, stop times, agencies, frequencies, and shapes; covers formal and informal ground operators.

District accessibility layer

654 district boundaries with population, employment, and accessibility indicators (jobs reachable within 60 min).

Origin–Destination (OD) survey

Trip origins, destinations, trip purpose, mode, and travel demand patterns — feeds mobility-flow features used in the KMeans candidate-area model.

Data Preparation

Cleaning decisions & correlation policy.

The cleaning phase translates the raw EDA findings into consistent analysis-ready inputs without changing the project questions. Each decision is traceable to a specific signal in the raw EDA.

Issue (from EDA)Preparation action
Text inconsistencies (spacing, case, punctuation)Trim strings, standardize text, normalize matching keys.
Station-name variants across linesNormalized names + physical-station IDs using name + rounded coordinates.
Raw spatial files in geographic CRSKeep EPSG:4326 for Folium; reproject to metric (EPSG:3857) for distance ops.
GTFS numeric fields read as textConvert stop_sequence, headway_secs, route_type, shape sequence, coordinates.
GTFS route geometry stored as ordered shape pointsSort by shape_id + sequence, build LineStrings, cut edge segments where possible.
Missing values in descriptive fieldsReport missing-rate; do not rely on nonessential fields (e.g. Arabic names).
OD missing trip-purpose, many categorical levelsPreserve for frequency analysis; aggregate into demand features only where KMeans uses them.

Correlation policy

Correlation heatmaps are not used for the raw EDA. Many numeric-looking columns are identifiers, sequence numbers, or generated keys — correlating them produces numeric output with weak planning meaning.

Instead the EDA relies on frequency tables, value counts, missing-value rates, spatial bounds, numeric summaries for valid continuous fields, and later cluster-validation metrics (silhouette, Davies-Bouldin) for the KMeans feature matrix.

Why this matters

Every cleaning action is traceable to a specific raw-EDA signal — making the pipeline auditable and rerunnable when new data arrives.

Limitations & Future Use

What the system can do when fresh data lands.

The pipeline is built to be refreshed. Every input is replaceable; every output regenerates deterministically from the source files.

Known limitations

  • Inputs are point-in-time snapshots (rail status, GTFS, OD survey, district pop/employment).
  • Correlation heatmaps deliberately omitted from raw EDA — identifier fields make them uninformative.
  • KMeans clustering is interpretive (typology), not predictive; results depend on feature selection.
  • Spatial thresholds (station-to-route, transfer distance) are tuned conservatively.
  • Arabic station-name field is 69.8% missing — relied on English name + coordinates.

Future use if updated data is available

  • Refresh GTFS files to update ground route coverage, stop sequences, and agency composition.
  • Replace OD survey lines to refresh demand patterns, trip-purpose distributions, informal trip-share features, and clustering inputs.
  • Update rail route and station files as projects move from planned to operating status.
  • Update district population and employment layers to keep accessibility-gain estimates aligned with new urban growth.
  • Compare alternative future network scenarios, station-placement options, or feeder-route redesign proposals.
  • Extend the dashboard with temporal GTFS service frequency, travel-time weighting, cost assumptions, and equity indicators when reliable data is available.
